Introduction of Project

Motivation

Public transit access is increasingly relevant in cities, particularly for metropolitans like Chicago. Besides the convenience they bring, they also define neighborhoods with transit-oriented development and reshape local socioeconomic and demographic profiles. By choosing Chicago as a case study, the following report investigates whether households place more value on transit-rich neighborhood (TODs) compared to other neighborhoods (Non-TODs). To identify TODs, the study creates a 1-mile unioned buffer from CTA L Line points, and classify neighborhoods with centroids located within the buffer as TODs.

Data sources for this study include 2009 5-Year American Community Survey and 2019 5-Year American Community Survey. Additional shapefiles, including Chicago municipal boundary and CTA L Line station locations, are downloaded from Chicago Data Portal (https://data.cityofchicago.org/).

Getting 5-Year ACS data for Cook County 2009 and 2019

The study retrieves the following variables from 5-Year American Community Survey (ACS) 2009 and 2019 for inspection: B25026_001E: total population in occupied housing units, B02001_002E: White alone, B15001_050E: Female Bachelor, B15001_009E: Male Bachelor, B19013_001E: Median Household Income, B25058_009E: Median Contract Rent, B06012_002E: Total Poverty by place of birth.

After the variables are retrieved, they are converted to Chicago’s CRS: NAD83 StatePlane Illinois East FIPS 1201 (EPSG 26971) (ESRI:103270).

5-Year ACS 2009

invisible(capture.output({
tracts09 <-
  get_acs(geography = "tract",
          variables = c("B25026_001E","B02001_002E",
                        "B15001_050E","B15001_009E",
                        "B19013_001E", "B25058_001E",
                        "B06012_002E"), 
          year=2009, state=17,
          county=031, geometry=TRUE)%>% 
  st_transform('ESRI:103270')

tracts09 <- 
  tracts09 %>%
  dplyr::select( -NAME, -moe) %>%
  spread(key = variable, value = estimate) %>%
  rename(TotalPop = B25026_001, 
         Whites = B02001_002,
         FemaleBachelors = B15001_050, 
         MaleBachelors = B15001_009,
         MedHHInc = B19013_001, 
         MedRent = B25058_001,
         TotalPoverty = B06012_002)

tracts09 <- 
  tracts09 %>%
  mutate(pctWhite = ifelse(TotalPop > 0, Whites / TotalPop, 0),
         pctBachelors = ifelse(TotalPop > 0, ((FemaleBachelors + MaleBachelors) / TotalPop), 0),
         pctPoverty = ifelse(TotalPop > 0, TotalPoverty / TotalPop, 0),
         year = "2009") %>%
  dplyr::select(-Whites,-FemaleBachelors,-MaleBachelors,-TotalPoverty)
}))

Relating L Line and Chicago Census Tracts

A 1-mile buffer around each L Line station is created and unioned as L Line’s transit corridor. Note: NAD83 StatePlane Illinois East FIPS 1201 measures distance in meters.

Creating L Line Buffer

invisible(capture.output({
st_crs(Lline)

Lline_Buffer <- st_union(st_buffer(Lline, 1609.344))
}))

Assuming that the centroids of each census tract is most representative of community’s average activity center, the study categorizes every census tract with its centroid contained by the L Line buffer to be a TOD, while the rest are Non-TODs.

Locating TODs and Non-TODs

ChicagoTracts.group <- 
  rbind(
      st_centroid(ChicagoTracts)[Lline_Buffer,] %>%
      st_drop_geometry() %>%
      left_join(ChicagoTracts) %>%
      st_sf() %>%
      mutate(TOD = "TOD"),
    st_centroid(ChicagoTracts)[Lline_Buffer, op = st_disjoint] %>%
      st_drop_geometry() %>%
      left_join(ChicagoTracts) %>%
      st_sf() %>%
      mutate(TOD = "Non-TOD")) %>%
  mutate(MedRent.inf = ifelse(year == "2009", MedRent * 1.19, MedRent),
         MedHHInc.inf = ifelse(year == "2009", MedHHInc * 1.19, MedHHInc))

Plotting spatial-temporal change in variables

The following section visualizes spatial-temporal differences between variables by TOD/Non-TOD categorization and year (2009 and 2019).

Locating TODs and Non-TOD with reference to CTA L Line stations

Fig.1 indicates where TODs and Non-TODs locate with reference to CTA L Line stations. It suggests that a greater number of TODs concentrate around central Chicago, particularly the Loop area. Comparatively, most suburban tracts are classified as Non-TODs.

1. Median Rent

Fig.2 shows that in both 2009 and 2019, TODs tend to have higher tract-level median rent, especially for tracts in downtown and north Chicago. Temporally, median rent has also increased more significantly in TODs than Non-TODs between 2009 and 2019. These observations indicate that higher rents in TODs are continuously reinforced.

ggplot() +

2. Median Household Income

3. Total Population

Fig. 4 indicates a general pattern of decreasing population in Chicago between 2009 and 2019, particularly for Non-TODs. While total population differences between tracts were more evident in 2009, the pattern events out in 2019. As a result, while higher populations live in Non-TODs compared to TODs in 2009, the difference is less significant in 2019. The Loop area, however, by ranking top under the population metric in 2019, has observed exceedingly high population growth. These observations indicate that more people are moving from Non-TODs to TODs.

Indicator Table

ChicagoTracts.summary <- 
  st_drop_geometry(ChicagoTracts.group) %>%
  group_by(year, TOD) %>%
  summarize(
    Rent = mean(MedRent.inf, na.rm = TRUE),
    Population = mean(TotalPop, na.rm = TRUE),
    Household_Income = mean(MedHHInc, na.rm = TRUE),
    Percent_Bach = mean(pctBachelors, na.rm = TRUE)
  )

ChicagoTracts.summary %>%
  unite(year.TOD, year, TOD, sep = ": ", remove = T) %>%
  gather(Variable, Value, -year.TOD) %>%
  mutate(Value = round(Value, 3)) %>%
  spread(year.TOD, Value) %>%
  kable() %>%
  kable_styling(full_width = FALSE, position = "center") %>%
  add_header_above(
    header = c("Table 1. Differences between variable means by Non-TOD and TOD census tracts, 2009-2019." = 5),
    align = "center",
    escape = FALSE
  ) %>%

    general_title = "\n",

  )

Observations from both Table 1 and fig. 6 correspond with previous discussions, as ToDs have higher average household income, share of bachelors, and rents than Non-TODs. These differences also grew sharper between 2009 and 2019. As average population of TODs has surpassed Non-TODs, they also indicate that more people are moving to TODs from Non-TODs.

In conclusion, the study confirms a general pattern that TODs are valued more by Chicago communities. This pattern is most outstanding near the Loop area in downtown Chicago.
